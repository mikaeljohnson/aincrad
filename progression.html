Progression Philosophy

Progression should feel earned. Every point in a skill should represent real time and real choice.

SAO's skill system was elegant in its simplicity: skills leveled by use, each player could hold a limited number, and mastery in one area meant deprioritizing another. The result was specialization — a world where swordsmen, blacksmiths, scouts, and healers each had a role that others couldn't fully replicate. No one could be everything.

This is the design principle Aincrad inherits. Specialization over generalization. A player who tries to master everything will be worse at everything than a player who focuses. This creates interdependence — the best smiths need skilled resource gatherers. The best fighters need quality gear. The best scouts need safe routes to report back on.

The Civ genre understood this economically but not in terms of personal stats — your Minecraft character in Civcraft was largely flat, your power coming from gear and organization. Aincrad adds the RPG layer: individual character development that creates genuine diversity in player capabilities.

Core constraint: Players have a Skill Point cap. You cannot max every skill tree. Every build is a tradeoff. This is not a frustrating limitation — it is the feature. Skill caps create roles, and roles create community.

The Skill System

How Skills Work

Skills are leveled through use. Mining stone levels your Mining skill. Killing enemies with a sword levels your Sword skill. Brewing potions levels your Alchemy skill. There are no quests to grind for skill points — you improve at what you do.

Each skill runs from Level 1 to Level 1000 (the SAO tradition). Each level provides a small stat bonus or unlocks a new ability. The first 100 levels come quickly. The next 400 are steady. Levels 700–1000 require serious dedication — they represent the mastery that defines a player's identity on the server.

Skill Points & The Cap

Each player has a Skill Point cap of 2,000. Total skill levels across all your skills cannot exceed this number. To invest more in one skill, you must reduce investment somewhere else (skills can be reset, but with a cost and a cooldown). This means:

  • A player who maxes two skills at 1000 each has no points left for anything else
  • A well-rounded generalist might have six to eight skills at moderate levels
  • Most players will settle into a personal build after some experimentation

Skill Inheritance

When a player dies and respawns, their skills are retained — death does not reset progress. Skills are the part of your character that is permanent. This is the counterbalance to the harsh death economy (gear loss on death) — you always keep your mastery, even when you lose everything else.

Combat Skills

Melee

One-Handed Sword

The classic archetype. Balanced offense and defense. The most common combat skill and the most developed in terms of high-level techniques.

Bonuses: Damage, attack speed, parry window
Capstone (Lv1000): Dual Blades (unique)
Melee

Two-Handed Sword

High damage, slower attack. Crushes armor. Excels at breaking shields and pushing through reinforced enemies. Vulnerable to fast opponents.

Bonuses: Damage multiplier, armor penetration
Capstone: Cleave (AoE swing)
Melee

Dagger

Speed and stealth. Low damage per hit, extreme attack rate. The assassin's weapon. Pairs with the Hiding skill for ambush builds. Critical strike mechanics.

Bonuses: Attack speed, crit chance, bleed
Capstone: Shadow Step (short blink)
Melee

Polearm

Extended reach. Keeps enemies at distance. Excellent for group formations — multiple polearm users create an effective front line that's hard to breach.

Bonuses: Reach, slow on hit, formation bonus
Capstone: Phalanx Stance
Ranged

Bow

Classic ranged combat. Long draw time but powerful. Essential for siege support and picking off enemies before melee engagement. High floor, very high ceiling.

Bonuses: Draw speed, damage, range
Capstone: Charged Shot (3x damage)
Ranged

Crossbow

Pre-loaded burst. Lower sustained rate than Bow but faster first shot. Better in ambushes and close quarters. The choice for dungeon explorers.

Bonuses: Reload speed, damage, knockback
Capstone: Rapid Load (instant reload)
Defense

Shield

Damage reduction, block mechanics, bashing. Pairs with any one-handed weapon. The tank archetype. Irreplaceable in organized group combat.

Bonuses: Block rate, bash stun, damage reduction
Capstone: Iron Will (brief invulnerability)
Utility

Hiding

Reduces visibility to enemies. At high levels, enables brief invisibility in shadowed areas. The core of the stealth archetype. Requires standing still to activate at low levels.

Bonuses: Detection reduction, movement while hidden
Capstone: Shadow Cloak (full invisibility)
Utility

Parry

Reduces damage taken when correctly timing blocks. Rewards mechanical skill — the best parry players take dramatically less damage than average. Hard to master, transformative at high levels.

Bonuses: Damage reduction, counter-attack window
Capstone: Perfect Parry (reflect damage)

Crafting & Trade Skills

Crafting

Smithing

The most economically powerful skill. High-level smiths produce the best weapons and armor on the server. Cannot be fully substituted by any other skill. Master smiths are among the most valuable players in any guild.

Bonuses: Item quality tiers, unlock recipes
Capstone: Legendary Forging
Crafting

Alchemy

Potions, poisons, and alchemical reagents. Support role for combat through buff potions. Damage role through poison applications. Economic role through selling consumables.

Bonuses: Potion potency, duration, batch size
Capstone: Master Elixir
Crafting

Tailoring

Cloth and light armor. Produces the best light armor for stealth and mage builds. Also creates banners, flags, and Covenant insignia — the aesthetic layer of the game.

Bonuses: Light armor quality, special fabrics
Capstone: Masterwork Garb
Crafting

Cooking

Underestimated. High-level cooks produce food items with powerful combat buffs. A good cook in a raiding party is a force multiplier. Cooking also drives the economy of agricultural floors.

Bonuses: Buff potency, saturation, effects
Capstone: Feast of Champions
Gathering

Mining

Ore yield, rare mineral discovery, tunneling speed. The backbone of the crafting economy. Deep miners discover resource deposits others miss. The most foundational gathering skill.

Bonuses: Ore yield, rare finds, speed
Capstone: Master Prospector
Gathering

Herbalism

Rare plant harvesting, yield bonuses, identification of alchemical properties. Feeds directly into Alchemy. Essential for players building the potion economy on early floors.

Bonuses: Yield, rare herb discovery
Capstone: Flora Sense (hidden herbs visible)
Trade

Merchant

Discounts from NPC shops, better NPC sell prices, access to rare NPC stock. The economic specialist. Low combat value, high economic value. Pairs with Smithing to create the ideal shopkeeper build.

Bonuses: Buy/sell rates, exclusive stock access
Capstone: Trade Magnate

Life Skills

Exploration

Cartography

Map creation and accuracy. High-level cartographers produce the most detailed, accurate maps of new floors — sold to guilds scouting expansion. A niche but valued profession.

Bonuses: Map detail, range, reveal speed
Exploration

Acrobatics

Fall damage reduction, climbing speed, parkour movement. The scout's essential life skill. High-level acrobats reach locations others physically cannot. Invaluable for reconnaissance.

Bonuses: Fall reduction, climb speed, wall grab
Social

Leadership

Passive bonuses to nearby Covenant allies. Aura effects that buff group performance. The command archetype. A high-level Leader running alongside a combat group makes everyone better.

Bonuses: Group damage, defense, morale aura
Social

Fishing

Yield and rare catch bonuses. Access to floor-specific aquatic resources. Peaceful skill but economically meaningful on aquatic floors. The contemplative player's choice.

Bonuses: Catch rate, rare items, special bait

Titles & Achievements

Titles are permanent public recognitions displayed alongside a player's name. They cannot be purchased or earned through grinding — only through specific significant achievements.

TitleAwarded ForRarity
Frontliner Participated in a Floor Boss first clear Uncommon — one per floor, up to 24 players
Slayer of [Boss Name] Delivered the killing blow to a Floor Boss Unique — one player per boss, ever
The Black Swordsman Reach Level 1000 in One-Handed Sword and complete a boss solo Unique or near-unique — most prestigious combat title
Grandmaster Smith Reach Level 1000 in Smithing Rare — maybe 5–10 on the server at any time
The Cleared Player who achieves the final boss kill and clears all 100 floors Server-defining — the first player to achieve this ends the era
Nation Founder Founded a Covenant that reached 50+ members Uncommon — maybe 10–20 over the server's life
The Chronicled Participated in a server event considered historically significant by player consensus Rare — awarded by player vote after major events

Gear Progression

The Death Economy

When a player dies in the field or in combat, they drop a portion of their inventory. This is the hard edge of Aincrad. The exact percentage is a tuning decision (starting suggestion: 30% of items dropped on death, chosen randomly from non-equipped gear), but the principle is non-negotiable: death has a material cost.

This is why good gear matters. This is why the economy exists. This is why you don't fight recklessly. The best-geared players have the most to lose.

Equipped gear exception: Items currently worn (armor, held weapon) are NOT dropped on death — only inventory items. This prevents a single death from stripping a player bare. It is punishing but not devastating.

Gear Tiers

TierSourcePower LevelNotes
Common Basic crafting, NPC shops Baseline Where everyone starts. Adequate for Floors 1–3.
Uncommon Skilled crafting, Field Boss drops +20% The standard for active adventurers. Mid-floor capable.
Rare Master smiths, Floor Boss drops +50% Sought after. Worth fighting over. Names begin to matter.
Unique First-clear Boss drops, specific lore quests +100% Named items. One-of-a-kind. The player who has this sword is known for it.
Legendary Grandmaster smith + unique materials + mastery +150% May not exist at launch. The theoretical ceiling. Server events when one is created.

Named Items

Unique and Legendary items have permanent names and are tracked in the server Chronicle. A sword with a history — carried by a famous player, used to kill a Floor Boss — accumulates lore. Players who acquire famous items inherit their reputation. Enemies will know what you carry.

The New Player Experience

The hardest design challenge: making the game welcoming without making it soft. New players must be able to enter the world, get their footing, and find a place — without the experience being trivial or patronizing.

The Newcomer's Grace

New players (accounts less than 72 hours old) receive Newcomer Status:

  • No item drop on death for the first 72 hours
  • Visible marker (small icon) that signals to other players they are new — attacking them carries extra karma cost
  • Access to a free starter kit from the main town registry on Floor 1: basic iron gear, a small amount of Col, and a Teleport Crystal
  • A brief tutorial dungeon available from Floor 1's main town that explains the core systems

Predator behavior: Players who repeatedly target Newcomers despite the karma cost will face escalating social consequences. The karma system handles most of this organically. Habitual new-player predation is one of the fastest ways to earn Red status and get hunted by the server community.

The Guild Invitation System

Established Covenants can designate themselves as Recruitment Open, making them visible to new players on the main town notice board. New players looking for a home can browse active guilds, see their reputation and floor activity, and send join requests. This onboarding pipeline is crucial — a new player who joins a guild in their first session is 10x more likely to stay than one who logs in alone.
